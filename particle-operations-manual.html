1. What Is Particle Health?

Particle Health is a company that built a single connection point to almost every medical record system in the United States. Instead of calling 50 different hospitals and faxing 50 different request forms, you make one API call (think of it like one search query) and Particle goes out and finds that patient's records across the entire country.

Imagine Google, but instead of searching websites, it searches every hospital, clinic, pharmacy, and lab in America for one specific patient's medical history. You type in a name and birthday, and 12 seconds later you have their complete health records.

They cover approximately 320 million+ patients across 160,000+ healthcare organizations. That's roughly 90% of all electronic health records in the US.

2. Why Should Anyone Care?

The Problem Today

When you walk into a new doctor's office, they have almost zero information about you. Your old doctor's records? Sitting in a different computer system that doesn't talk to this one. The ER visit you had last year in another state? Gone. Your medication list from your pharmacy? Not here.

So what happens? The front desk faxes (yes, in 2026, healthcare still runs on fax machines) a request to your previous providers. That takes 3-5 business days. Sometimes the records never arrive at all. Meanwhile, the doctor is making decisions about your health with incomplete information.

What Particle Fixes

Particle connects to all the major health data networks in the country. When a clinic uses Particle, they can pull your complete medical history — every medication, every lab result, every diagnosis, every surgery — in seconds. Not days. Seconds.

It's the difference between a detective spending 3 weeks interviewing witnesses one by one, vs. having access to every security camera in the city instantly. Same information, but the speed changes everything.

4. How a Patient Lookup Actually Works

When you search for a patient in Particle, here's exactly what happens behind the scenes. The whole thing takes about 12-30 seconds.

  1. You Send the Search

    Your system sends Particle a patient's basic info: first name, last name, date of birth, city, and state. That's it — 5 fields.

  2. Particle Updates Its Index

    The system checks its master patient database (called an eMPI — electronic Master Patient Index) to make sure it has the latest info on this person.

  3. Location Analysis

    Based on where the patient lives (and any hints about where they've been treated), Particle's algorithm figures out which hospitals, clinics, and pharmacies to check.

  4. Hundreds of Mini-Searches Fire Off

    Your one search gets split into potentially hundreds of sub-queries — each one pinging a different healthcare organization. "Hey, do you have records for Jane Smith, born 3/15/1978?"

  5. Networks Respond

    Each organization that has a match sends back their records. This happens across Carequality, CommonWell, Surescripts, and regional networks simultaneously.

  6. Identity Verification

    Particle double-checks that the records coming back actually belong to the right person. Name, DOB, and demographics are cross-referenced to prevent mix-ups.

  7. Data Cleanup

    Raw records get parsed, deduplicated (removing copies), normalized (standardizing codes), and formatted. A 50% data gap rate gets reduced to about 17%.

  8. Packaging

    Everything gets bundled into your preferred format — could be a clinical document (C-CDA), modern web format (FHIR), or simple structured data (Flat JSON).

  9. Delivery

    Your system receives the complete patient record. Labs, meds, allergies, problems, encounters, vitals — everything, from everywhere, in one response.

6. The Legal Stuff

You can't just look up anyone's medical records for fun. There are strict rules about why you're accessing the data. This is called a "Purpose of Use" (POU).

The 3 Reasons You Can Access Records WITHOUT Patient Permission

Under HIPAA, healthcare providers can access records for these reasons without asking the patient first. These are called TPO:

Treatment

The most common reason. A doctor needs to see a patient's history to provide care. "I'm treating this patient and I need their medication list so I don't prescribe something dangerous." This is what we use at MyPulseScan.

Payment

Insurance companies verifying claims, processing reimbursements, checking if a procedure was medically necessary. "We need to confirm this patient had the diagnosis that justified this surgery before we pay the claim."

Operations

Quality audits, risk adjustment, policy development. "We need to review records across our patient population to measure care quality." Not used for individual patient care.

When You DO Need Patient Permission

Individual Access (Patient Request)

When a patient wants to see their own records or share them with someone. Requires two things: (1) the patient explicitly consents, and (2) you verify their identity. This is newer — enabled by the Cures Act anti-information-blocking rules.

Important: Your Purpose of Use must be supported by BOTH Particle AND the specific health network you're querying. Not every network supports every purpose. Treatment is the most universally supported.

8. Getting Connected

Here's what it takes to go from "I've never heard of Particle" to "I'm pulling patient records."

  1. Sign the Contract

    Work with Particle's sales team. Agree on pricing (per-patient-per-month for Signal, per-query for Workbench, etc.) and which products you need.

  2. Get Your Credentials

    Particle gives you a client ID and secret. These are like a username and password for their API. You'll use OAuth 2.0 (industry standard login protocol) to authenticate.

  3. Complete Network Questionnaire

    The health data networks need to know who you are and WHY you're accessing records. You fill out forms about your organization, your Purpose of Use, and your compliance setup.

  4. Delegation of Authority (DAuth)

    If you're accessing data on behalf of a healthcare provider (like we do — clinics use OUR platform to access records), you need a delegation agreement. The provider formally authorizes you to query on their behalf.

  5. Build in Sandbox

    Particle has a test environment with fake patients. Build and test your integration here without touching real data. Test patients have realistic but completely fabricated records.

  6. Go Live

    Once your integration passes testing and your compliance paperwork is approved, you switch from sandbox to production. Now you're querying real records.

9. How They Clean Up the Data

Raw medical records from hundreds of sources are messy. Different hospitals code things differently, use different formats, and sometimes send duplicate records. Particle runs the data through 5 cleanup steps:

  1. Parsing

    Converts raw clinical documents (XML files) into structured data you can actually use. Turns a 50-page document into organized categories: medications, labs, problems, etc.

  2. Imputation

    Fills in gaps by connecting related records. If Hospital A has a diagnosis but no lab results, and Lab Corp has the results, Particle links them together. Reduces data gaps from ~50% to ~17%.

  3. Deduplication

    Removes duplicates. If 3 different hospitals all report the same medication, you see it once — not three times. Reduces duplicate records by up to 90%.

  4. Normalization

    Standardizes how things are coded. Hospital A might call it "Lisinopril 20mg" while Hospital B calls it "lisinopril hctz 20/12.5mg tablet." Particle normalizes these to standard medical codes so they're comparable.

  5. Deltas

    If you've queried this patient before, Particle can show you only what's NEW since your last query. Instead of re-reading 200 records, you see just the 3 new ones. Huge time saver for ongoing care.

11. Common Questions

Is this legal? Can you just look up anyone's records?

Yes, it's legal — but only for approved purposes. Healthcare providers can access records for Treatment, Payment, or Operations under HIPAA without patient consent. We use it for Treatment. You cannot look up your ex's records out of curiosity — that's a federal crime.

Does the patient know their records are being pulled?

For Treatment purposes, no explicit notification is required (same as when a hospital requests records via fax today). However, patients always have the right to request an accounting of disclosures.

What if the records are wrong?

Particle doesn't create records — it retrieves what already exists in other systems. If a hospital has wrong data, Particle will pull wrong data. The Record Validator catches some errors (like records for the wrong person), but clinical accuracy is the source system's responsibility.

How fast is it really?

Most queries return data in 12-30 seconds. Complex cases with records at dozens of facilities might take longer. Some data (especially from smaller clinics) might trickle in after the initial response.

Does it work for EVERY patient?

About 90% of US patients have records in connected systems. Some gaps exist: very small rural clinics, patients who've only seen cash-pay providers, or records in systems that haven't joined the national networks yet.

What does it cost?

Pricing varies by product and volume. Signal is priced per-patient-per-month (PMPM). Workbench is typically per-query. Volume discounts are significant — our rates from Particle range from $1.00/patient/month at low volume to $0.15/patient/month at 1M+ patients. Minimum contract: $50K/year.

Can Particle data be used for RPM billing?

No — not directly. RPM (Remote Patient Monitoring) requires actual FDA-cleared devices streaming physiological data from the patient's home. Particle provides historical records, not real-time device data. However, Particle data helps IDENTIFY which patients qualify for RPM programs and provides the clinical foundation for enrolling them.

What's the difference between Particle and an HIE?

A Health Information Exchange (HIE) is typically regional — it connects hospitals in one state or metro area. Particle connects to multiple HIEs AND the national networks simultaneously. It's like the difference between having one local library card vs. having access to every library in the country through one card.
